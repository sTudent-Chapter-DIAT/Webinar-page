Pendulum Dynamics and Spin Hall Effect of Light

When light is absorbed in a semiconductor, it can create bound pairs of electrons and holes called excitons. Placing these excitons in a tiny optical cavity allows them to strongly couple with photons, forming hybrid light-matter particles known as exciton-polaritons. These particles behave like ultra-light quasiparticles and can undergo Bose-Einstein condensation, a quantum state where many particles act as one. In this talk, the speaker presents experiments on polariton condensates confined in a ring-shaped microcavity. They engineered a rigid pendulum-like potential where a "pendulum made of light" was observed. The swinging motion of this light pendulum and its gradual decay were measured by detecting the escaping photons. These oscillations provided insights into polariton-polariton interactions. Surprisingly, some excitons traveled farther than expected—an effect attributed to unique polariton dynamics. In another experiment, a nonequilibrium polariton gas was created in the pendulum trap. Due to polarization-dependent cavity splitting, a pseudo-magnetic field emerged, giving rise to the optical spin Hall effect. Similar to the spin Hall effect in electrons, polariton spins caused spatial polarization separation. These findings shed light on hybrid light-matter behavior and advance the path toward polariton-based photonic circuits for quantum technologies.

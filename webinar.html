Pendulum Dynamics and Spin Hall Effect of Light

When light is absorbed in a semiconductor, it can create bound pairs of electrons and holes called excitons. If we place these excitons in a tiny optical cavity, they can strongly mix with photons, forming hybrid light-matter particles called exciton-polaritons. These polaritons behave like very light particles and can undergo Bose-Einstein condensation—a collective quantum state where many particles behave as one. In this talk, I will present experiments on polariton condensates confined in a ring-shaped microcavity. We engineered a rigid pendulum potential, where we observed a pendulum made of light. We observe how this light pendulum swings back and forth and gradually comes to rest by detecting the photons that escape from the cavity. By studying these oscillations, we measured how strongly polaritons interact with each other. Interestingly, we also discovered that some excitons could travel much farther than expected, showing a surprising "polariton effect" on their motion. In another experiment, we created a nonequilibrium polariton gas inside this pendulum trap where the polarization dependent cavity splitting leads to a pseudo-magnetic field in the cavity. Much like how the spin-1/2 electrons show the spin Hall effect, here the light-like polaritons showed a similar effect known as the optical spin Hall effect, where their spin leads to a separation of polarizations in space. These studies offer new insights into the behavior of hybrid light-matter particles and bring us closer to building polariton-based circuits for processing optical information in future quantum and photonic technologies.
